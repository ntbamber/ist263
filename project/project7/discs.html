Disc Types

Overview

Discs are an important part of the game, and each one has a specific role. Disc golfers use different discs for different scenarios depending on if you need more distance, control, or accuracy. Most beginners will be able to play with just a fairway driver, mid-range, and putter, but your bag will most likely grow as you grow in the sport.

Disc Types and How They’re Used

Drivers

These discs are built for maximum distance. With a sharp edge and short but thick rim, drivers cut through the air and travel far. They’re best for long throws off the tee, but the thick rim can make them harder for beginners to control.

Typical Speed Range: 9 - 15

Recommended Distance Drivers for Beginners: Innova Gorgon, Discraft Avenger SS, MVP Trail
Fairway Drivers

A step down in speed from distance drivers. Easier to control and more predictable—great for beginners or for shots that need accuracy over pure distance.

Typical Speed Range: 6 - 9

Recommended Fairway Drivers for Beginners: Innova Leopard, Latitude 64 River, Axiom Crave
Mid-Range Discs

Versatile for medium-distance throws. A balance of distance and control and perfect for approaches. Many players use mid-ranges as their go-to discs when learning.

Typical Speed Range: 4 - 6

Recommended Mid-Range Discs for Beginners: Discraft Buzzz, Innova Roc, Axiom Hex
Putters

The slowest but most accurate discs. Designed for short throws and putting. Also useful for controlled approaches when you don’t want extra glide.

Typical Speed Range: 1 - 4

Recommended Putters for Beginners: Innova Aviar, Dynamic Discs Judge, Latitude 64 Pure

Understanding Flight Numbers

Most discs show four numbers (e.g., 9 / 5 / -2 / 1) that summarize how the disc is intended to fly. Treat these as a comparison guide, not exact physics—they’re based on ideal throws and can vary by thrower, plastic, weight, wind, and elevation. The goal is to help you compare molds and pick what fits your throw. (Assume a right-hand backhand, RHBH, unless noted.)

What is RHBH? A right-hand backhand means a right-handed player grips the disc across the chest, pulls across the body, and releases in front. For RHBH throws, “turn” describes early movement to the right and “fade” describes the disc’s late finish to the left.

  • Speed: how much power the disc needs to fly as designed.
  • Glide: how well the disc stays aloft.
  • Turn: early-flight right movement (RHBH).
  • Fade: late-flight left finish (RHBH).

How to use this in real life: start with lower Speed, neutral-to-understable Turn, and moderate Glide to match typical beginner power. As your form grows, move into faster or more overstable options.
